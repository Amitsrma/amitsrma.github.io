Homeworks are a part of student life, something a student cannot not have. As opposed to classwork, which a student does when in a classroom, Homeworks are supposed to be done at home. Homeworks are supposed to keep a student busy studying, learning, re-learn the topics taught in class, get better at them, and be a better student. Strangely, I and my classmates, after grade 10, did not get almost any Homeworks; grades 11, 12, 13-14-15-16 (four years of bachelors) were relaxing years. I cannot remember doing homework. There were once-a-week labs whose reports would be due every second week or at the end of the semester. I now want to make a self-absorbed comment, "I did pretty good as a guy who did not do any homework".

If you are curious about the schools that you should get into so your burden of homework will be taken care of, they are..; on second thought, I think this is not the right moment to disclose. I will do so later in this blog. The point I am trying to make is not that a student might end up well-off despite Homeworks but that the environment they study in plays important role in their learning, rather than Homeworks. I firmly believe that is the reason who I am today.

!["Growing Curiosity!"](Image/homeworks/homework-02-curiosity.jpg){: style="float:center; width=100%"}

It started with St Xavier's College, at Maitighar in Kathmandu. I was told, that was the college to get into if you want to have a firm foundation towards what or who you want to be. I, an ignorant little kid who wanted to become a Mechanical Engineer, had just chosen physics to be my discipline because that was what I was supposed to study to become a ME. I am on the right path, got in a good college, took physics and I am already doing laboratory stuff. Then, in one of my first labs, a teacher dropped the truth. He said that the college I was in was not as awesome as one can hear about it in public. It was just like any other colleges, the teachers there were not the ones who made that college great. It had and does have a good reputation because of the awesome and hardworking students from all around the country that choose to study there. This meant everything was on me rather than the teachers and I would not be successful by just being there. In hindsight, I am kind of glad I did not understand that the teacher meant I have to study harder and be better than I was, I am glad that I was still discovering and taking it one day at a time.

We did not have homework. Teachers would complete lessons, summarize them, tell us what we should understand by now, and inform of us of topics we should practice. My friends were silly, crazy smart, and hard-working. Everyone seemed like a problem solver, a prodigy, ambitious, openminded, and many more. I still remember the time when a mischievous classmate, who was not paying attention in class and was busy chatting with others; the teacher noticed, and he challenged the student that they will not be able to summarize what he would teach next and if the student does successfully summarize, he will not have to leave class. To our and teacher's disbelief, that mischievous kid passed the challenge with flying colors (mischievous kids who are secretly smart?). Maybe I am a nerd, I remember such events with outrageous details. To be honest, I might be a nerd. I used to sit around and listen to the discussions about general and special relativity, the fourth state of matter, quantum mechanics (something I still understand as the matter being in particle and waveform simultaneously) while solving the problems related to that day's thermodynamics or calculus class. Initially, had no idea about the topics unrelated to the day's class but eventually, I learned about them, and oh boy, is time dilation interesting!

!["Naturally Curious!"](Image/homeworks/homework-03-naturally-curious.jpg){: style="float:center; width=100%"}

We did not need homework. There was curiosity, energy to work to satisfy that curiosity, and friends who asked interesting questions to fuel that curiosity, helping to push further. How can one ignore the calculus problem that asked you to calculate the optimal velocity of a person walking in rain such that they would get the least wet, or finding the volume of an ellipse? It is easier to follow through difficult challenges when you are doing something you like with others who share the same propensities.

It was similar in grade 13-14-15-16 (the bachelor's degree). The difference was that bachelor's was more hands-on. I was studying the discipline I wanted to, Mechanical Engineering. It was exciting, I was on the way to learn how to build the robocop's car that looked like Delorean with cool gadgets. We did projects and labs that required understanding the process, get hands dirty to put sensors together, collect data, analyze, build, and report but, we did not have any homework. University was freer than college, teachers, and professors did not force us to do anything. It was all up to us. Classes were not mandatory, except some; syllabus, suggested books, and other resources were public information, one could study engineering without going to lectures and just attending labs because I had already experienced that one can practice theory on their own. I went to classes either to keep up with what was being taught or to clarify on confusion I had about topics being taught. It was here where I learned how useful sines and cosines, first and second derivatives, integrations were. It was challenging, confusing at times but we had a syllabus, a final exam, and an engineering degree that everyone who was there, wanted.

I think that is why we did not need Homeworks.
